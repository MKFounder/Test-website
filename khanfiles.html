01
The shelter journey
Five shelters. Three years. One worsening story.
5 min read

Mohammad Khan entered Toronto's emergency shelter system in June 2023. He was homeless, preparing to start university, and depending on a system that is supposed to provide not just a roof but a genuine pathway to stable housing. What he found instead was a network of facilities where his complaints were ignored, his records were constructed against him, and his attempts to assert his rights were met with escalating punishment. By January 30, 2026, with a windchill of −30°C and four separate legal proceedings pending, the City of Toronto discharged him into the street.

This is the documented journey across five facilities — all drawn from the City's own 454 pages of MFIPPA-disclosed records and the 189-paragraph amended Statement of Claim filed in Ontario Superior Court (CV-25-00754505-0000).

02
Racial & homophobic harassment
The N-word. The F-word. Homes First said nothing could be done.
4 min read
Pleaded in Ontario Superior Court · CV-25-00754505-0000 · Paragraphs 6–7 + 91–92

Between July and November 2023, residents at 4117 Lawrence Avenue East subjected Mohammad Khan to repeated racial and homophobic harassment — including slurs such as the N-word and F-word, and other degrading abuse. Staff told him there was nothing they could do.

The amended Statement of Claim pleads that Khan "made numerous complaints to staff and emailed management." The response from Homes First staff: they dismissed or minimized his concerns, warned him that defending himself physically could lead to his own discharge — not the harassers' — prevented him from documenting incidents, and failed to investigate or intervene.

The claim further pleads at paragraph 92 that "the discriminatory and retaliatory patterns the Plaintiff experienced at Homes First, including repeated exposure to racial and homophobic slurs such as the 'N word' and 'F word,' staff stating that nothing could be done about this conduct, form part of the factual context relevant to the Defendants' breach of their duty of care." Khan's litigation hold notice describes the period as one of "repeated racial epithets ('N----' and 'F----') and intimidation by a group of residents, management inaction until Homes First CEO was emailed."

A City supervisor — Richard Brown at Travellers Hostel — later acknowledged in writing that Khan had been subjected to "racialized targeting, name-calling, and physical assault." His response was to redirect Khan to the same disputed case management channel. No investigation was initiated. No human rights referral was made. No incident report was generated. The claim pleads this as Charter section 15 and negligence, and that the supervisor's written acknowledgment without follow-up constitutes "institutional ratification of discriminatory treatment."

Note: The slurs are described using conventional dashes in this publication. The full words are pleaded verbatim in the Statement of Claim filed in Ontario Superior Court (CV-25-00754505-0000) and in the litigation hold notice (MFIPPA AP-2026-00032, p. 397).

This pattern — where the person who complains faces consequences, not the person who caused the harm — repeats across every facility. At Lawrence Avenue, staff warned Khan about consequences. At Placer Court, the manager blamed Khan. At Fort York, staff encouraged Khan to move rather than addressing the roommate he had complained about for weeks. That roommate later assaulted him.

◆ WHAT THE TORONTO SHELTER STANDARDS REQUIRE

The Toronto Shelter Standards impose mandatory obligations regarding non-discrimination, client safety, and fair complaint handling. The claim pleads that every operator named as a defendant breached these standards. At Homes First, the pleading at paragraph 91 states that "Homes First's conduct breached the standard of care and reflected discriminatory and reprisal-based treatment contrary to the principles underlying the Ontario Human Rights Code, including by failing to respond appropriately to racial and homophobic harassment directed at the Plaintiff and by discouraging him from asserting his rights."

03
July 3, 2024 · Fort York Residence
Punched at 5am. Staff refused 911. Interrogated that same morning.
4 min read

On July 3, 2024, at approximately 5:00 a.m., the roommate Mohammad Khan had been complaining about for weeks physically assaulted him, striking him multiple times in the head. The assailant admitted the assault to staff. Staff refused to call 911. Khan called himself. Paramedics transported him to St. Michael's Hospital, where he was diagnosed with a concussion. He returned to Fort York the same morning. He was immediately summoned to a meeting.

Despite Khan having just returned from hospital with a diagnosed concussion, supervisor Sean Shipley pressed ahead with the meeting. Colleague Julio Castillo was present. Khan's request to bring a notebook was refused. His request to postpone was refused.

⚠ DOCUMENTED THREAT · ON THE RECORD

During the meeting, Sean Shipley made statements the Plaintiff understood as threatening: words to the effect that staff would "take care of" Khan "in a different way" — while looking directly at him. Julio Castillo then repeated: "yeah, we're going to take care of you in a different way" — while typing at a computer. Khan did not know notes were being created in real time, without his knowledge, for use in future administrative decisions affecting his shelter access.

The incident report authored that morning — by Janelle Brathwaite — described Khan as "irate," "ranting," and staring at staff. It classified causation as "Undetected mental health for both clients" — treating the assault victim identically to his attacker in the same field. It recommended a 30-day Restriction of Service against Khan. Although dated July 3, the report referenced a July 4 follow-up — indicating it was supplemented after the fact, then presented as a contemporaneous record.

The claim pleads that this assault was foreseeable. Khan had filed at least eight prior complaints about this specific roommate. Staff had repeatedly failed to act. When the foreseeable violence occurred, the institutional response was to interrogate the victim while constructing records framing him as the problem — records that would then be used to justify removing him from the program.

Instead of neutrally investigating the assault and accommodating the Plaintiff's medical condition, Fort York staff created and maintained records that framed the Plaintiff as the problem and laid groundwork for his removal from the program.

During the September 4 meeting, Shipley asked how long it would take Khan to move "if you had to leave at 1:30." Khan understood 1:30 p.m. Neither Shipley nor Spiteri specified a.m. At 1:30 a.m. the following morning — less than 24 hours later — shelter staff directed Khan to pack immediately for transfer to Maxwell Meighen Centre. Refusal meant discharge from the entire Toronto shelter system. Khan had been told weeks earlier he would not be transferred until October. He had provided his university timetable in good faith based on that assurance. His university classes the next morning were missed.

He arrived at Maxwell Meighen to no intake staff, a bedbug-infested room, non-functioning washrooms, residents smoking substances indoors, and a staff comment that the facility was "for troublemakers." The City's records logged his departure from Fort York as a "voluntary transfer."

Spiteri is pleaded to have created records attributing blame for the assault to Khan, prepared a discharge letter with a retroactive date, and used her recordkeeping authority adversely. Shipley is pleaded to have conducted an adversarial record-producing meeting with a concussed resident, made coercive threats, and contributed to a punitive overnight transfer.

05
The secret file
The database that follows residents — invisible to them, used against them.
5 min read

Every person in Toronto's emergency shelter system has a file in SMIS — the Shelter Management Information System. It follows them from facility to facility. Case notes, incident reports, service plans, mental health characterizations — all of it stored, searchable, accessible by staff at any shelter. The resident is not shown what is written. They are not given copies in real time. They cannot see how they are characterized while those characterizations shape decisions about their housing.

◆ WHAT IS SMIS?

SMIS — Shelter Management Information System is the City of Toronto's central database for its emergency shelter network — the largest municipal shelter system in Canada. Every intake record, case note, incident report, and service plan for every shelter resident is stored here. Staff at any City-funded shelter can access a resident's full record. The resident sees nothing unless they file a formal MFIPPA (freedom of information) request. Khan's 454 pages came through this process. An IPC audit complaint is now asking whether this violates Ontario privacy law for all shelter residents.

Khan's SMIS file shows a progression the claim calls "upstream record propagation." Before he arrived at Fort York, his file already contained a notation entered at a previous Homes First shelter recording he had ADHD and PTSD. Khan had never disclosed either. He denied both. The source assessment form was not attached. The notation was there anyway — shaping how Fort York staff read him before he arrived.

At Travellers Hostel in July 2025, his intake form recorded: Mental health concerns — No. Diagnosis — No. Four months later, his ISP asserted a "mental health condition" that "has caused some limitations which disallows him from actively engaging in a housing search." No assessment occurred between those entries. No diagnosis was made. No clinical event is documented in 454 pages. His legal name is spelled wrong on the same document.

Contradiction in City Records · Travellers Hostel 2025 EVIDENCE C
Intake form (July 2025): "Mental health concerns: No. Diagnosis: No."
ISP dated November 28, 2025: "Clients mental health condition has caused some limitations which disallows him from actively engaging in a housing search."
Time between entries: approximately four months. Clinical assessments in that period: zero. Diagnoses made: zero.
CITY-DISCLOSED · AP-2026-00032 · PAGES 215–216 AND RELATED SMIS CASE NOTES

A City-disclosed employment invoice from March 2024 documents Khan working as a Business Instructor at $28/hour. A SMIS case note written six months later asserts he "has not made any effort to secure employment." The City's own disclosure contradicts the City's own notes.

On December 18, 2025 — the same day Khan filed a formal MFIPPA records request — a SMIS note appeared containing discharge-adjacent language absent from his own contemporaneous recap email sent hours earlier. The claim identifies six convergence points where adverse action followed protected activity within 24 to 72 hours — each timestamped in the City's own records.

06
The OSAP question
Provincial student aid used as a shelter discharge rationale. Twice. Two years apart.
4 min read
The OSAP Thread · What every homeless student in Ontario needs to read

Ontario's student financial aid is not housing. It does not provide accommodation. Receiving OSAP does not mean a person has somewhere safe to sleep. So why did the City of Toronto use it — twice — to discharge a homeless student from emergency shelter?

OSAP — the Ontario Student Assistance Program — is the provincial government's main financial aid for post-secondary students. It provides grants and loans. A person can receive OSAP while in a shelter. OSAP is income. It is not a roof.

▸ INSTANCE 1 · SEPTEMBER 4, 2024 · FORT YORK

Leianne Spiteri's discharge letter stated Khan had "elected to pursue full time education," would be receiving OSAP, and had "made himself ineligible for the program." No mention of his concussion. No mention of the July 3 assault. Government student aid treated as evidence he no longer needed emergency shelter.

▸ INSTANCE 2 · JANUARY 16, 2026 · TRAVELLERS HOSTEL

Janeen Alexander emailed: if post-secondary education was his "primary focus," he would "not be eligible for the homeless shelter program." Khan replied in writing the same day: "OSAP does not constitute housing, nor does it render a person ineligible for emergency shelter or shelter-based housing pathways." The same logic. A different year. A different shelter. A different manager.

▸ WHY DOES THIS MATTER BEYOND ONE PERSON?

There is no written policy in the Toronto Shelter Standards making OSAP receipt a ground of ineligibility for emergency shelter. If this is an unwritten operational assumption applied across the system, it affects every homeless student in Toronto who receives student financial aid. They would not learn about it until the discharge letter arrived. The IPC complaint asks whether this is systemic. The claim pleads the OSAP rationale as part of a discriminatory pattern based on receipt of public assistance and race.

◆ WHAT IS OSAP?

OSAP — Ontario Student Assistance Program is the Province of Ontario's main student financial aid program. It provides grants and loans to eligible students in post-secondary education based on financial need. OSAP is specifically designed to support people who would otherwise not be able to afford education — including people in unstable housing. Receiving OSAP while in a shelter is not only legal, it is the intended use case. Using OSAP receipt as a shelter ineligibility criterion would effectively punish homeless students for pursuing the educational pathway that government policy holds up as the route out of poverty.

08
January 30, 2026
−30°C. Four proceedings active. No housing pathway. The discharge went ahead.
4 min read

On January 30, 2026 — with the windchill in Toronto at −30 degrees Celsius — Richard Brown, City supervisor at Travellers Hostel, issued a formal discharge notice against Mohammad Khan. The stated basis: non-compliance with mandatory in-person housing-focused case management. Khan had been engaging in writing. The City refused to accept written engagement. No accommodation was offered or explored. No less intrusive alternative was considered before the discharge was issued.

⚠ WHAT WAS SIMULTANEOUSLY TRUE AT THE MOMENT OF DISCHARGE

Khan's HRTO application for an interim remedy to prevent this exact discharge was still pending before the Tribunal.

An active IPC complaint was under review questioning the City's SMIS data practices.

An Ombudsman Toronto file was open.

A civil action in Ontario Superior Court (CV-25-00754505-0000) was active.

The COHB — the only housing pathway the shelter system had been managing for Khan — had closed to new applicants nine days earlier.

No alternative housing had been identified.

The windchill outside was −30°C.

The claim pleads that Khan repeatedly clarified he was not refusing housing-focused engagement — he was requesting it proceed in writing while disputed records remained unresolved. Manager Janeen Alexander's response: "Your proposal to engage in case management in writing only is not acceptable." No accommodation was offered. The claim pleads this as a failure of the duty to accommodate under the Human Rights Code, as Charter-breaching conduct, and as part of the retaliatory pattern documented throughout the six convergence points.

◆ WHAT IS COHB · AND WHY DID ITS CLOSURE MATTER?

The Canada-Ontario Housing Benefit (COHB) is a portable rent subsidy funded jointly by federal and provincial governments. Access runs through shelter case management — you must be engaged with your shelter caseworker to receive a COHB referral. When Alexander confirmed on January 21 that COHB had "ended and is not accepting new applications," the only housing pathway the City had been managing for Khan closed. Nine days later, he was discharged with no alternative. The claim pleads this sequence as evidence that the discharge was not a housing outcome — it was a punishment for asserting procedural rights.
